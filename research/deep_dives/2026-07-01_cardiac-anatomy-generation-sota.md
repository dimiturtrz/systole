# Cardiac Anatomy Generation for Synthetic MRI Label-Map Synthesis

**Date**: 2026-07-01
**Context**: Project systole — synthetic cardiac MRI label-map generation for training data augmentation
**Status**: settled

## TL;DR

Three practical approaches exist: (1) **parametric shape models** (UK Biobank HeartSSM, MRXCAT2.0) offer interpretable anatomy control but require hand-tuned pathology parameterization; (2) **learned generative models** (VAE-GAN, diffusion) capture realistic anatomical diversity but suffer mode collapse and require large labeled datasets; (3) **hybrid approach** (MRXCAT2.0 + biophysical simulation) couples statistical shape learning with tissue texture, enabling pathology-specific ground truth but requires 2 hours compute per image. Topology preservation (RV-myo-LV spatial validity) remains unsolved for all — most rely on post-hoc mesh repair or shape-prior constraints rather than end-to-end topology guarantees.

## Question 1: Cardiac Statistical Shape Models (SSM) — Construction & Public Availability

### Construction Methods
UK Biobank's **HeartSSM** model [S1] constructs a 3D+t statistical shape model via:
- **Image segmentation**: Cardiac chamber contours extracted across 95,695 participants' full cardiac cycles
- **Principal Component Analysis (PCA)**: Shape variance decomposed into principal components (PCs)
- **Result**: 128-dimensional shape descriptor capturing 83.6% of population shape variance in the first 11 PCs
- **Incremental PCA**: Trains on 64,000 synthetic 3D+t four-chamber meshes generated from training segmentations [S1]

The **Auckland Cardiac Atlas Project** (Young, Nash) [S2] uses:
- **Prolate spheroidal coordinate parameterization**: 215 parameters to represent LV geometry (enables efficient computational modeling)
- **Guide-point modeling**: Interactive customization of 16 bicubic finite elements with C1 continuity fitted to epicardial/endocardial surfaces [S2]
- **Mode-function analysis**: Principal components associated with clinical indices (heart size, sphericity, mitral geometry) [S2]

### Sampling & Extrapolation
- Modes are **linear combinations of PCs** in PCA-based models; gaussian distribution assumed over latent space
- **Known limits**: Population-specific (UK Biobank skew toward healthy volunteers); Gaussian linearity assumption breaks for pathology extrapolation [S3]
- **Temporal resolution constraint**: Dynamic models limited by cardiac phase resolution in source imaging [S1]

### Public Availability
- **UK Biobank HeartSSM**: Representative volumetric meshes available at Zenodo; full UK Biobank data requires application but is open-access for health research [S1, S12]
- **GitHub**: Bai et al. open-source code for UK Biobank cardiac MR analysis at `github.com/baiwenjia/ukbb_cardiac` [S12]
- **Auckland Cardiac Atlas**: Public database at `www.cardiacatlas.org` with publications and imaging reference models [S2]
- **UK Digital Heart Project**: Statistical Shape Model publicly available at GitHub (`UK-Digital-Heart-Project/Statistical-Shape-Model`) [S1]

### Mode extrapolation IS done, and there is a ready, CC-BY dataset (added post-review, HIGH)
The Rodero/King's College four-chamber SSM cohort [S19] is the concrete instantiation of "extrapolate PCA
modes for coverage": meshes are generated by **adding ±1/±2/±3 SD of each PCA mode to the average mesh**
("extreme2"/"extreme3" cohorts) — i.e. deliberate mode-extrapolation to reach anatomy rare in the
sampled population. Assets on Zenodo, all **CC-BY-4.0** (usable):
- `zenodo.org/records/4593739` — 39 four-chamber meshes: 1 average + extreme1/2/3 (±SD of modes 1–9), VTK
  unstructured grids with fibers + universal ventricular coordinates.
- `zenodo.org/records/4506930` — 1000 synthetic four-chamber meshes sampled from the SSM (adult healthy).
- `zenodo.org/records/4590294` — the 20 base CT-derived healthy meshes the SSM was built from.
- 2021 method paper (Rodero et al., PLOS Comp Biol) + a 2025 sex-/disease-specific virtual-cohort extension [S19].
This is the single most directly-reusable "coverage-by-construction" asset found — but it is **CT-derived,
four-chamber, healthy-population** (no MINF/HCM by construction; pathology still needs geometric knobs).

## Question 2: Parametric / Biomechanical Models — Pathology Parameterization

### Analytic / Parametric Models
**Prolate spheroid & ellipsoid LV models** [S2] parameterize shape with:
- Wall thickness (t)
- Cavity radius (r)
- Longitudinal/apical extent

These are computationally efficient (215 parameters for a full LV) but **do not inherently capture pathology** — pathology must be manually imposed.

### Pathology Parameterization (MRXCAT2.0 Reference Implementation)
MRXCAT2.0 [S6] parameterizes three disease states via **k-means clustering of VAE latent space** on cardiac anatomy:

| Condition | Parameterization |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Healthy (NOR)** | LV mass 85–140g, EF 34–51%, peak radial strain 0.45–0.95 |
| **DCM** | Enlarged blood pools, thin walls (LV mass at lower range) |
| **HCM** | Increased wall thickness (septal-dominant heterogeneity) [S8] |
| **Infarction** | Localized elliptical scar + automatic transition of wall thickness, mechanical, electrophysiological parameters between healthy/scar tissue [S6] |

Wall thickness heterogeneity is a **key HCM marker**: standard deviation of thickness across 16-segment LV significantly elevated vs. controls [S8]; thin wall infarction zones handled via gradient parameters [S6].

### Digital Twin Approaches
**Niederer/Nordsletten framework** [S5] scales to ~55,000 UK Biobank participants:
- **Atlas-based mesh**: Deforms template mesh to patient-specific segmentations (biventricular surface)
- **Volumetric FE mesh**: Tetrahedral conversion via Meshtool; distinct regions for LV myo, RV myo, aortic, mitral, tricuspid, pulmonary valves
- **Fiber personalization**: Rule-based myocardial fiber models estimated from sparse in-vivo cDTI (diffusion tensor imaging) data, interpolated to 3D via harmonic lifting [S13]
- **Current limitation**: Healthy cohorts only; disease parameterization left as future work [S3]

## Question 3: Learned Anatomy / Label-Map Generation — Methods & Failure Modes

### SynthSeg (Diffeomorphic Deformation Mode)
SynthSeg [S4] generates label maps via:
- **Smooth diffeomorphic transformation**: Random vector field upsampled and integrated to ensure invertibility
- **Label consistency**: Target labels obtained by applying same deformation to training segmentation (ensures anatomy and labels stay aligned) [S4]
- **Cardiac application**: Brain-trained model applied to cardiac segmentation using 13 randomly drawn MMWHS label maps resampled to 1 mm isotropic [S4]

### VAE-GAN Pathology Synthesis
**MELBA framework** [S9] combines VAE + GAN for cardiac MR generation:
- **VAE component**: Learns low-rank latent representation of epicardium/endocardium coordinates via convolutional variational autoencoder trained on MMWHS
- **Latent space sampling**: Three modes via k-means: healthy, DCM, HCM clusters (each cluster samples independently for pathology-specific generation)
- **GAN component**: Generates realistic MR intensity from cardiac shape + tissue parameters
- **Output**: Paired synthetic images + ground-truth segmentations with known pathology

**Advantages**: Captures realistic organ texture, pathological diversity; can generate >10k labeled samples autonomously [S9]

### Diffusion Models for Segmentation Mask Generation
**2D U-Net diffusion**: Self-attention captures global anatomical dependencies, residual blocks handle local detail; 3D consistency via slice-by-slice conditioning [S9]

### Failure Modes (Cardiac-Specific)
1. **Mode collapse**: GAN produces only R-peaks or cardiac feature subsets; VAE collapses to population mean (especially under sparse pathology clustering) [S11]
2. **Feature hallucination**: Generator invents anatomically implausible structures (e.g., wall intersections, false septa); missing clinically critical features [S11]
3. **Texture artifacts**: Checkerboard patterns, bleeding across chamber boundaries when GAN discriminator weakens on underrepresented pathologies [S9]
4. **Boundary violations**: Myocardium intrudes into chambers or vice versa; requires post-hoc masking or learned discriminator supervision [S9]

## Question 4: Parametric vs Learned — The Generalization Tradeoff

### Evidence on OOD Robustness & Segmenter Generalization

**Parametric sweep (domain randomization) benefits**:
- Fully randomized contrast + resolution in synthetic training allows segmentation of real scans across wide target domains **without retraining** [S7]
- Domain randomization emphasizes **data heterogeneity over authenticity** to promote generalization across variations never explicitly observed [S7]
- Synthetic signal randomization can exceed real-world variation, achieving **performance on par or exceeding networks trained on real data** [S7]

**Learned generation benefits**:
- Pathology synthesis approaches can generate **realistic images with relevant pathological diversity**, serving as realistic substitutes for real MR [S7]
- Fine-tuning foundation models on synthetic cardiac data yields **Dice 0.908–0.961** on test sets, competitive with real-data training [S10]
- **Learned models show superior generalization to severe pathology** (congenital heart disease, extreme DCM): models trained on one dataset (ACDC) generalize across datasets with completely different distributions [S10]

### The Tradeoff — Direct Quote & Mechanism
*"Domain randomization allows synthetic signal randomization to increase beyond what is typically observed in real-world data, achieving performance on par or exceeding networks trained with real data. In contrast, pathology synthesis approaches can generate realistic images with relevant pathological diversity for training cardiac segmentation networks that generalize well to unseen diseases."* [S7]

**Mechanism**: Domain randomization gains **robustness via coverage** (any contrast/artifact possible); pathology synthesis gains **semantic understanding** (realistic anatomy+texture teaches discriminative features). **Verdict**: Both approaches are complementary, not competing. Parametric sweep covers acquisition variability; learned generation covers anatomical/pathological variability. **Hybrid strategies** (randomize + learn) likely optimal but not yet benchmarked head-to-head in cardiac literature.

## Question 5: Surrounding Anatomy (Whole-FOV) — XCAT/MRXCAT, Availability, Limitations

### MRXCAT (Original, 2014)
**Framework**: Extended Cardiac Torso phantom (XCAT) + MR physics simulation [S5, S16]

**Anatomical Coverage**:
- **Cardiac detail**: Blood pool, myocardium of atria/ventricles divided into 8 partitions; arteries, veins, pericardium as separate compartments [S16]
- **Torso anatomy**: Based on Visible Human Project; includes lung, liver, chest wall implied but **not explicitly detailed** in primary paper [S16]
- **Parameters**: Spatial resolution, matrix size, temporal resolution, geometry, non-rigid motion adjustable [S16]

**Public Availability**: Available online at `www.biomed.ee.ethz.ch/mrxcat` [S16]

**Limitations**:
- Cannot simulate individual k-space line timing (computational constraint)
- Uses signal equations (not Bloch equations); excludes B0 inhomogeneity and magnetization evolution
- **Lacks organ texture and heterogeneity** — pure label phantoms [S16]

### MRXCAT2.0 (2023)
**Advances**: Couples statistical shape model + biophysical simulation + tissue texture generation [S6, S14]

**Whole-Organ Approach**:
- **LV anatomy**: Statistical shape model (VAE-learned from MMWHS) provides epicardium/endocardium
- **Surrounding anatomy**: XCAT torso phantom still used for non-cardiac structures
- **Texture**: Procedurally generated via signal texture model

**Pathological Coverage**: Healthy, infarcted, dilated, hypertrophic LV function with ground-truth EF, mass, strain [S6, S14]

**Computational Cost**: ~2 hours per slice for full simulation (biophysics + image generation); anatomy generation alone takes seconds [S6]

### Whole-Thorax Label-Map Generation — Current State
**Dedicated approaches**:
- **RadGenome-Anatomy** [S15]: Large-scale anatomy-labeled chest radiograph dataset via physically grounded volumetric projection; includes cardiac, pulmonary, hepatic, osseous labels [S15]
- **Whole-heart refinement frameworks** (U-Net parcellation) [S15]: Extrapolate from 6-label (basic chambers) to 10-label (refined great vessels, atrial appendages) segmentation
- **TotalSegmentator/BOA models** [S15]: Full-volume anatomical predictions for thorax including all surrounding organs

**Gap**: No single **cardiac+lung+liver label-map generator** integrates realistic pathology parameterization with surrounding anatomy constraints. MRXCAT2.0 covers LV pathology + XCAT torso, but torso is generic (not personalized). Whole-thorax generators (TotalSegmentator) lack cardiac pathology tailoring.

## Question 6: Topology & Plausibility — Methods for Keeping Anatomy Valid

### The Problem
Deformation-based approaches are **prone to topological violations**, particularly:
- Membrane penetration (myocardium intersects RV free wall or septum)
- Unnatural inter-chamber connections (e.g., LV cavity intrudes into RV via shared wall)
- Invalid septal geometry (RV septum and RV free wall intersect) [S17]

### Solution 1: Topology-Preserving Mesh Loss (TPM Loss) [S17]
**Mechanism**:
- Explicitly identifies **topology-violating points** during mesh deformation
- Integrates topological constraints at the **mesh level** (not post-hoc)
- Enforces **spatially consistent reconstructions** via penalty on intersection violations

**Results**: Reduces topology violations **up to 93.1%** while maintaining DSC 89.1–92.9% [S17]

**Application scope**: Recent work (2025) on CT/MRI meshes; cardiac-specific validation ongoing.

### Solution 2: Relational Anatomical Supervision [S17]
- Explicitly supervise shared surfaces between anatomical structures (e.g., **wall between LV and RV** represented as distinct region in template mesh)
- Each shared surface modeled as unified boundary with constraints preventing interpenetration

### Solution 3: Atlas-Based Constraints (Current Practice)
- Deform **template mesh** fitted with anatomical structure constraints to patient-specific segmentations
- Template enforces gross topology; refinement is gradient-based optimization respecting template structure [S3, S5]
- **Known failure**: Can still produce RV septum/free-wall intersections during conversion to volumetric FE mesh; requires post-hoc mesh repair [S3]

### Solution 4: Shape-Prior Constraints (Parametric Models)
- Restrict deformations to **PCA subspace** of training anatomies (all training anatomies are valid by construction) [S1, S2]
- **Tradeoff**: Constrains generalization; cannot extrapolate far beyond training population covariance

### Conclusion: **No End-to-End Guarantee**
Most production systems rely on **layered constraints**: (1) shape priors (PCA subspace), (2) anatomical landmarks/supervision, (3) post-hoc mesh repair. **TPM Loss** is the first learning-based end-to-end approach, but cardiac-specific validation is limited.

## Parametric vs Learned — Verdict from Evidence

| Aspect | Parametric (MRXCAT2.0, SSM) | Learned (VAE-GAN, Diffusion) |
|--------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| **Anatomical diversity** | Limited to PCA subspace; extrapolation breaks linearity | Captures full training distribution + interpolations |
| **Pathology specificity** | Hand-tuned parameters (DCM dilation, HCM thickness); transparent | Learned from data; opaque; mode-collapse risk on rare pathologies |
| **OOD generalization** | Good for acquisition variation (domain randomization) | Good for unseen patient populations (if training data diverse) |
| **Topology validity** | Shape priors enforce (but can fail post-hoc); PCA guarantees are by construction | No inherent guarantee; TPM Loss mitigates 93% of violations |
| **Computational cost** | Fast (seconds for anatomy; MRXCAT2.0 ~2h for full sim) | Data generation cheap (real-time once model trained); training cost high |
| **Ground truth** | Biophysically simulated (knows EF, strain exactly) | Heuristic (learned from labels; no independent validation) |
| **Public availability** | MRXCAT2.0, UK Biobank HeartSSM, Auckland Atlas | MMWHS, ACDC, HVSMR-2.0 datasets; no pre-trained cardiac models released |

**Synthesis**: For **systole MVP**, a **parametric-first approach** (MRXCAT2.0 or UK Biobank HeartSSM + custom pathology parameterization) is recommended: start with known-valid anatomy, transparent parameter space (LV mass, EF, strain ranges), and biophysical ground truth. Learned augmentation (VAE-GAN on top of parametric base) can follow once baseline label distribution is understood.

## Recommendation for Systole Version-A Build

### Architecture
**Tier 1 (MVP: Parametric Base)**
1. **Start with MRXCAT2.0 or UK Biobank HeartSSM** to generate base anatomy
2. **Parameterize pathology** via latent space interpolation (healthy → DCM/HCM endpoints on learned VAE clusters) or manual control (LV mass, EF, strain ranges)
3. **Generate label maps** via diffeomorphic deformation (SynthSeg-style) or template mesh fitting
4. **Ensure topology** via shape-prior constraints (PCA subspace) + post-hoc mesh validation

**Tier 2 (Beta: Hybrid + Whole-FOV)**
1. Couple parametric LV anatomy with XCAT torso phantom for **whole-thorax label maps** (cardiac + lung + liver + fat)
2. Train VAE-GAN on (parametric anatomy, realistic texture) pairs to learn **mode diversity** beyond manual parameterization
3. Add **TPM Loss** during mesh refinement to enforce topology constraints end-to-end
4. Validate on MMWHS/HVSMR-2.0 segmenter generalization (OOD cardiac pathology test sets)

### Why This Order
- **Parametric first**: Transparent parameter space (no mode collapse), biophysical ground truth (known EF/strain), no large labeled dataset required
- **Deformation via diffeo**: Preserves topology better than arbitrary neural deformations; aligns with SynthSeg success in brain
- **Hybrid second**: Once baseline anatomy is solid, learned augmentation adds diversity without losing interpretability

### Key Caveats
1. **Pathology parameterization is manual**: No automatic DCM/HCM generation; requires domain expertise to set wall-thickness/cavity-radius trade-offs
2. **Whole-FOV is unsolved**: No existing system integrates cardiac pathology + lung/liver anatomy; will require custom XCAT parameterization or post-hoc organ placement
3. **Topology is fragile**: Even shape priors can fail post-hoc; TPM Loss is new (2025) and not yet widely validated on cardiac meshes
4. **Ground truth is noisy**: MRXCAT biophysical sim is detailed but excludes B0 inhomogeneity, complex fiber geometry; don't treat simulated anatomy as absolute truth

## Confidence & Gaps

### What This Research Covered (HIGH CONFIDENCE)
- ✅ **SSM construction methods**: PCA + mesh alignment (UK Biobank, Auckland)
- ✅ **Public assets**: HeartSSM, MRXCAT2.0, MMWHS, HVSMR-2.0 all confirmed open-access or apply-to-use
- ✅ **Parametric pathology parameterization**: MRXCAT2.0 explicit (DCM/HCM/infarct via VAE clustering + manual adjustment)
- ✅ **Topology violations**: Recent TPM Loss paper (2025) shows 93.1% reduction; earlier systems rely on post-hoc repair
- ✅ **Domain randomization vs pathology synthesis**: Literature consensus (both complementary, not competing) [S7]

### What This Research Did NOT Answer (Open Questions)

1. **Quantitative OOD comparison**: No direct head-to-head benchmark of parametric-sweep vs learned-generation on cardiac segmenter generalization. Literature discusses both; no unified evaluation.

2. **Whole-FOV pathology integration**: How to parameterize lung-cardiac coupling (e.g., RV overload affecting lung compression)? XCAT + MRXCAT don't natively couple cardiac→pulmonary physiology.

3. **Fiber geometry in synthetic anatomy**: MRXCAT2.0 generates shape+texture; does it include personalized fiber orientation for biophysical simulation? Paper mentions biophysical model but not fiber detail.

4. **TPM Loss on cardiac meshes**: Topology-Preserving Mesh Loss validated on CT/MRI generic anatomy; **no published cardiac-specific validation** (only 2025-era preprints).

5. **Failure mode quantification**: Mode collapse in cardiac VAE-GAN not explicitly studied; GAN reviews cited general mode collapse but not cardiac-specific anatomy artifacts.

6. **Segmenter generalization to extreme pathology**: Learned models generalize to unseen populations [S10]; do they generalize to **extreme parameter ranges** (e.g., EF <20%, massive wall thickness 50mm)? Unknown.

### Recommended Follow-ups
- **Build & benchmark**: Implement MRXCAT2.0 parametric baseline; train U-Net on synthetic labels; test OOD on real MMWHS/HVSMR-2.0
- **Fiber geometry**: Contact ETH Zurich (MRXCAT team) to clarify fiber parameterization in biophysical model
- **TPM Loss validation**: Adapt TPM Loss to cardiac mesh generation; measure topology violation rate on generated pathologies
- **Whole-FOV coupling**: Model RV/LV interaction with lung field-of-view (requires computational fluid dynamics or phenomenological lung compliance model)
