### Q1 · Why is EF (a ratio) trustworthy despite partial-volume error? — 4/5
**Answer:** most boxes are internal; the error is a *surface* effect — bad at low
resolution, fine at high. And in the 40–80% EF range a small boundary error won't
tip the clinical scales.
**Grade:** Right on surface-vs-bulk and resolution. **Missed the main reason:**
**correlated-bias cancellation** — a consistent (proportional) error hits EDV and
ESV the *same* way, so it divides out in `(EDV−ESV)/EDV`; multiplicative bias cancels
exactly, additive cancels in the numerator (stroke volume). And the failure case:
when ED/ES errors *differ* (HCM: tiny thick-walled ES cavity) → no cancellation.
*(Minor: it's the **cavity/endocardial** boundary that matters, not the myocardium.)*

### Q2 · If you wanted true mL (not just EF), what breaks? — 2.5/5  ⚠ weakest
**Answer:** need infinite resolution + the volume of muscle to subtract from the
empty space.
**Grade:** Resolution point right (partial volume, esp. through-plane). **Wrong
operation:** we segment the **cavity (blood) directly** — nothing to subtract.
"Subtract muscle" is **LV mass** (epicardium − cavity), a different number. Also
missed: consistent **basal-plane** rule, **apical-cap** model, **papillary-muscle**
convention. True mL needs those, not subtraction.

### Q3 · Why is through-plane error ≫ in-plane error? — 4.5/5
**Answer:** the slices have much better lateral (in-plane) resolution than the gap
between successive slices; complementing with vertical (orthogonal) slices could
offset the error.
**Grade:** Correct — the between-slice axis (10 mm) is undersampled vs in-plane
(1.56 mm). And the fix maps to **real practice**: long-axis (2-/4-chamber) views are
acquired to pin valve plane + apex; orthogonal/multi-view (or isotropic 3D cine)
constrains through-plane shape. Strong.

### Q4 · Marching cubes: why isovalue 0.5, and what changes with a non-binary field? — 4/5
**Answer:** use the U-Net probabilities; the surface cuts a cube edge where an
"out" corner meets neighbours that aren't — keeping 0.5 lets you make the triangle
that cuts the edge. *(self-flagged as a rough explanation)*
**Grade:** The **edge-crossing** instinct is correct. **Punchline missed:** the
vertex position on the edge is **linear interpolation** of the two corner values.
Binary (0/1) → crossing at the **midpoint** (blocky). Probability (e.g. 0.2/0.8) →
crossing slides **off-midpoint → sub-voxel smoothing**. That's why you'd mesh the
*probability* field, not the hard mask. 0.5 = the decision boundary.

### Q5 · Why fewer vertices (4,842) than voxels (12,104)? — 4.5/5
**Answer:** vertices are the voxel corners; with multiple objects it's hard to blow
the voxel budget — not all pixels are boundary.
**Grade:** Right reason — **only boundary cubes generate geometry**; fully-in or
fully-out cubes make none, so vertices live on the **shell**. **Vertex count ∝
surface area; voxel count ∝ volume** (the surface-vs-bulk theme again). *(Fix:
vertices sit **on the edges** at the interpolated crossing, not *at* corners.)*

### Q6 · Could you marching-cubes a smoother input to fix the stepping — honest? — 5/5
**Answer:** the voxel info is our information frontier; beyond it you're either
guessing, or using a trained statistical model that will likely build it right-ish.
**Grade:** Nailed it. Smoothing/upsampling = **interpolation = a smoothness prior,
no new information** (honest as cosmetics, dishonest sold as resolution). A learned
**shape/super-res model** = a **principled guess** (hearts are regular), better than
naive interpolation but **still inference, not measurement**. That's the honest line
and the **clinical-grade gap** (and G6, shape models).
